# Romania: Economic Trajectory and Structural Challenges
### A Data-Driven Analysis, 2000–2024

*Data sources: Eurostat, World Bank. Charts in `charts/`, raw data in `raw-data/`.*

---

## Executive Summary

Romania's economic story over the past two decades is one of remarkable growth shadowed by deep structural failure. GDP per capita (PPP) has risen from roughly 27% of the EU average in 2000 to 77% in 2024 — one of the fastest convergence trajectories in the bloc. Yet this growth has come at compounding costs: a fiscal deficit that has now reached **9.3% of GDP** (tied for the worst in the EU), a population that has fallen by more than 4 million since 1990, a social welfare system that leaves over a quarter of citizens at risk of poverty, and an institutional capacity that consistently fails to absorb the EU funds meant to correct these imbalances.

Romania is not a failing economy. It is a rapidly growing one with a structural architecture that is increasingly incompatible with that growth. The tensions are now acute, and the window for adjustment is narrowing.

## 1. Macroeconomic Overview

### 1.1 Growth Story

Romania has been one of the EU's fastest-growing economies since accession in 2007. Real GDP growth averaged above 4% per year from 2000 to 2019, with a peak of **8.2% in 2017** — driven partly by consumption-fuelled fiscal stimulus. Post-COVID recovery was strong at 5.6% in 2021, but the economy has since decelerated sharply: **2.3% in 2023 and just 0.9% in 2024**, the weakest reading since the pandemic year.

*→ Chart: `01a_gdp_growth.png`*

The deceleration reflects simultaneous headwinds: fiscal consolidation pressure, elevated inflation eroding real incomes, a cooling European demand environment, and the structural limits of a labour-scarce economy.

### 1.2 Convergence

The convergence chart is Romania's most compelling headline number. GDP per capita at PPP has climbed from 27% of the EU27 average in 2000 to **77% in 2024** — a near-tripling in relative living standards in one generation.

| Country | 2000 | 2010 | 2020 | 2024 |
|---------|------|------|------|------|
| Romania | ~27% | ~47% | 72%  | 77%  |
| Poland  | ~48% | ~63% | 78%  | 81%  |
| Bulgaria| ~28% | ~44% | 58%  | 66%  |

Poland and Romania started at similar absolute levels in 2000, but Poland's convergence has been both faster and more sustained. Romania surged in 2017–2019 on unsustainable fiscal stimulus, stagnated in 2020–2022, and has only modestly resumed convergence. The gap to Poland — a country with similar history and EU entry timing — has proved stubbornly persistent.

*→ Chart: `01b_gdp_convergence.png`*

### 1.3 Economic Structure

Romania's sectoral composition has evolved steadily from an agriculture-heavy post-communist structure toward a service-led economy, though industry retains a significant share. As of 2022:

- **Services**: ~56% of GDP
- **Industry**: ~28% of GDP
- **Agriculture**: ~4% of GDP (exceptionally high by EU standards — EU average is ~2%)

The large agricultural share reflects both Romania's natural endowment (it ranks among the EU's top agricultural producers) and the persistence of low-productivity subsistence farming. The industrial base, anchored by automotive (Dacia/Renault), chemicals, and an expanding IT sector, is the primary export engine.

*→ Chart: `01c_gdp_sectors.png`*

## 2. Public Finances: The Central Crisis

If there is a single defining economic problem in Romania today, it is fiscal. The general government deficit has been consistently among the EU's largest, and the 2024 figure of **−9.3% of GDP** — equal to the COVID-year 2020 figure — places Romania in a category of its own among non-wartime peacetime economies.

### 2.1 The Deficit Trajectory

Romania's fiscal problems predate COVID. The deficit was already at **−4.4% of GDP in 2019** — before any pandemic shock, and in a year of solid 4% GDP growth. The story since then:

| Year | RO deficit | PL deficit | CZ deficit |
|------|-----------|-----------|-----------|
| 2019 | −4.4%     | −0.7%     | +0.3%     |
| 2020 | −9.3%     | −6.9%     | −5.6%     |
| 2021 | −7.2%     | −1.7%     | −5.0%     |
| 2022 | −6.5%     | −3.4%     | −3.1%     |
| 2023 | −6.6%     | −5.2%     | −3.7%     |
| 2024 | −9.3%     | −6.4%     | −2.0%     |

Poland's 2024 deficit is elevated by extraordinary defence spending (approaching 4.7% of GDP) and is widely viewed as temporary. Romania's is structural: it has never returned to the Maastricht 3% threshold since 2016. Czechia, facing the same European headwinds, halved its deficit between 2023 and 2024.

Romania has been under the EU's Excessive Deficit Procedure (EDP) since 2020. The consolidation path agreed with the European Commission has been repeatedly revised because Romania has repeatedly missed it.

*→ Chart: `02a_deficit.png`*

### 2.2 The Revenue Problem

The deficit is partly a spending problem, but primarily a revenue problem. Romania collects just **28.7% of GDP in tax and social contributions** — the lowest rate in the EU, against an EU average of 40.2% and a eurozone average even higher. France collects 45.2%, Sweden 42.2%, even Slovakia manages 35.9%.

The causes are well-documented:
- **Large informal economy**: cash-economy sectors (construction, agriculture, retail) with systematic under-reporting
- **VAT gap**: Romania's VAT compliance gap is among the EU's largest — roughly 35–36% of theoretical liability goes uncollected
- **Structural under-investment in ANAF** (tax authority): digital infrastructure, audit capacity, and enforcement systematically underfunded
- **Multiple exemptions**: the agricultural sector, for example, is largely outside the formal tax base

*→ Chart: `02c_tax_revenue.png`*

### 2.3 The Spending Rigidity

On the expenditure side, the problem is structural inflexibility. Two items dominate:

1. **Pensions**: the 2019 pension law nearly doubled pension levels in real terms. Pension expenditure has risen from roughly 8% of GDP to over 10%, against an EU average closer to 12% — but in the context of Romania's low revenue base, even this is unsustainable. Reversing the 2019 law is politically near-impossible.

2. **Public sector wages**: a series of populist wage increases from 2016–2019 compressed the deficit headroom permanently.

The result is a government that spends around **41–42% of GDP** (broadly European-normal) but collects only **29% of GDP** in revenues — a structural gap of over 12 percentage points that no short-term measure can bridge.

*→ Chart: `02d_rev_vs_exp.png`*

### 2.4 Public Debt

Public debt stood at **54.8% of GDP in 2024**, up from 35% pre-COVID — a 20 percentage point deterioration in five years. It remains below the Maastricht 60% ceiling, but the trajectory is steep, and current deficit levels imply further rapid accumulation. Romania's borrowing costs have risen in response: long-term government bond yields have been among the highest in the EU for a non-programme country, reflecting market concern about fiscal sustainability.

*→ Chart: `02b_debt.png`*

## 3. Labour Market and Demographics

### 3.1 Employment

Romania's employment rate of **63.8%** (ages 15–64, 2024) is the lowest among its peer group. Czechia reaches 75.4%, Hungary 75.1%, Poland 72.5%. The gap — roughly 10 percentage points — represents hundreds of thousands of Romanians outside the formal labour market, many engaged in subsistence agriculture or the informal economy, many more who have emigrated.

*→ Chart: `03a_employment_rate.png`*

Unemployment at **5.4% in 2024** is not alarming in isolation. But low unemployment alongside low employment signals not a tight labour market but a shrunken workforce — people have left the labour force entirely, either through emigration or discouragement.

*→ Chart: `03b_unemployment.png`*

### 3.2 Youth Unemployment

Romania's youth unemployment rate (**23.9%** for ages 15–24 in 2024) is the most troubling single indicator in the labour market. It has been above 20% for most of the past decade, and rather than converging with peers, it has diverged:

| Country | 2019 | 2022 | 2024 |
|---------|------|------|------|
| Romania | 21.0%| 22.8%| 23.9%|
| Bulgaria| 12.1%| 10.6%| 12.3%|
| Hungary | 11.4%| 10.6%| 15.2%|
| Poland  |  9.7%| 10.8%| 10.8%|
| Czechia |  5.6%|  6.8%|  9.1%|

At nearly 24%, Romania's youth unemployment is more than double the Czech rate. This is not primarily a cyclical problem — it persists in boom years as well as slowdowns. It reflects a skills mismatch, an education system misaligned with labour market needs, and the emigration of the most mobile young Romanians before they even enter the domestic labour market.

*→ Chart: `03c_youth_unemp.png`*

### 3.3 Wages and Labour Costs

The Labour Cost Index (2020=100) tells a striking story. Romania's index reached **155.2 in 2024**, the fastest-rising in the peer group (Bulgaria: 163.8, Hungary: 166.0 — both also rapidly converging from very low bases). Poland stands at 153.5, Czechia at 120.3.

Romanian wages have risen sharply in EUR terms over the past decade, narrowing the cost advantage that attracted FDI. This compression is not inherently bad — it reflects genuine productivity gains and living standard improvements — but it requires matching improvements in productivity and skills to avoid competitiveness erosion.

*→ Chart: `03d_wages.png`*

### 3.4 Population Collapse

Romania's demographic trajectory is among the most severe in Europe. The population has fallen from an estimated **23 million in 1990 to 19.05 million in 2024** — a decline of nearly 4 million people, or 17%, in 35 years.

The driver is dual: very low birth rates (total fertility rate around 1.6) and massive emigration following EU accession. The 2007 accession opened free movement to Spain, Italy, Germany, and the UK. In the years that followed, Romania lost an estimated 3–4 million working-age citizens to Western Europe — the largest sustained emigration in post-war European history outside of conflict zones.

*→ Charts: `03e_population.png`, `03e2_romania_population.png`*

Remittances — money sent home by the diaspora — have stabilised around **2.5–3.1% of GDP** annually, representing a significant transfer that supports domestic consumption and reduces measured poverty. But remittances mask the deeper cost: the human capital that left does not return, and the care economy (child-rearing, elder care) has been severely disrupted by the departures.

*→ Chart: `03f_remittances.png`*

The age dependency ratio is rising — more elderly per working-age adult — and this pressure will intensify through the 2030s and 2040s as the large cohorts born before 1990 reach retirement age against a dramatically smaller working-age population.

*→ Chart: `03g_dependency_ratio.png`*

## 4. Inflation and Monetary Policy

### 4.1 From Hyperinflation to European Norm — and Back

Romania's inflation history is a textbook arc of post-communist monetary stabilisation. The 1990s saw inflation rates above 150% as price controls were lifted and the lei collapsed. The 2000s brought disinflation, and by the mid-2010s Romania was operating with inflation near the 2% European norm — sometimes even turning slightly negative (−1.1% in 2016).

The 2021–2023 global inflation shock hit Romania harder and more persistently than most peers. HICP inflation peaked at **12.0% in 2022**, fell to 9.7% in 2023, and reached 5.8% in 2024 — still well above the Euro Area average by that point. Critically, 2025 data shows **6.8%** — inflation is re-accelerating as fiscal stimulus flows back into the economy.

*→ Chart: `04a_inflation.png`*

### 4.2 BNR Response

The National Bank of Romania (BNR) began tightening in late 2021, raising its policy rate from 1.75% to a peak of **7.0% in 2023**. This was a significant and appropriate response. By 2024 the BNR had begun modest easing to 6.5%, but the re-acceleration of inflation in 2025 complicates the path.

A structural feature of Romanian inflation is its wage-push component: public sector wage increases and minimum wage hikes (which have been aggressive in recent years) feed through rapidly into services inflation.

*→ Chart: `04b_policy_rate.png`*

### 4.3 RON/EUR: The Managed Float

The RON/EUR exchange rate has depreciated slowly but relentlessly — from 3.34 in 2007 at EU accession to **4.975 in 2024**. The BNR manages a tight corridor: day-to-day volatility is very low, but the trend depreciation persists. This gentle depreciation partially offsets wage increases in EUR terms, maintaining some export competitiveness, but it also raises the cost of EUR-denominated public debt and undermines confidence in long-term savings.

*→ Chart: `04c_ron_eur.png`*

### 4.4 Euro Adoption: The Perpetually Deferred Target

Romania has nominally committed to euro adoption since accession but has never come close to meeting the Maastricht criteria simultaneously. The 2024 picture:

| Criterion | Romania | Limit | Status |
|-----------|---------|-------|--------|
| Inflation | 5.9% | ~3.3% | FAIL |
| LT Interest Rate | 6.8% | ~4.8% | FAIL |
| Budget Deficit | 7.9% GDP | 3.0% | FAIL |
| Public Debt | 52% GDP | 60% | PASS |

Three of four criteria are breached, and the deficit criterion is breached by a factor of 2.6. There is no credible near-term path to euro adoption. The political incentive to adopt has also weakened — the RON gives Romania a depreciation valve that euro membership would permanently close.

*→ Chart: `04d_euro_criteria.png`*

## 5. External Sector

### 5.1 Current Account: A Structural Deficit

Romania's current account deficit reached **−8.2% of GDP in 2024**, making it one of the EU's most exposed economies on external balance. The deficit has been structural and widening:

| Year | Romania CA | Poland CA | Czechia CA |
|------|-----------|----------|-----------|
| 2017 | −3.2%     | −1.2%    | +1.4%     |
| 2019 | −4.9%     | −0.3%    | +0.4%     |
| 2021 | −7.2%     | −1.3%    | −2.0%     |
| 2022 | −9.3%     | −2.3%    | −4.5%     |
| 2024 | −8.2%     | +0.3%    | +1.7%     |

Poland and Czechia have largely corrected their post-COVID external imbalances. Romania has not. A current account deficit of this size, sustained over multiple years, represents an ongoing dependence on external financing — primarily EU transfers and FDI — to balance the external accounts. The risk is not immediate default, but rising external vulnerability.

*→ Chart: `05a_current_account.png`*

### 5.2 Trade Balance

Romania runs a persistent trade deficit in goods, driven by the import intensity of domestic consumption and investment. Exports represent about **36% of GDP**, imports **42%**, giving a goods and services trade deficit of roughly −6 percentage points. The economy has become more open — trade as a share of GDP has risen from the low 40s to over 75% combined — but the import bill consistently exceeds the export base.

The export profile is led by automotive (Dacia/Renault, Ford Otosan), machinery and equipment, and an increasingly significant IT services sector. Agricultural exports are large in volume but relatively low in value-added.

*→ Charts: `05b_trade_balance.png`, `05d_ro_trade_openness.png`*

### 5.3 Foreign Direct Investment

FDI inflows have been volatile and are trending down: **1.9% of GDP in 2024**, compared to Poland's 2.2% and Czechia's 3.8%. The decline reflects several factors: wage convergence eroding the low-cost manufacturing advantage, infrastructure deficits that raise logistics costs, and growing political uncertainty. Romania attracted significant automotive and manufacturing FDI in the 2000s–2010s, but greenfield investment in the latest technology sectors (semiconductors, advanced manufacturing) has disproportionately gone to Poland and Czechia.

*→ Chart: `05c_fdi.png`*

### 5.4 EU Funds: The Absorption Problem

Romania has consistently been the worst or near-worst performer in the EU on cohesion funds absorption. For the 2014–2020 programming period, absorption stood at **72.4%** by end-2023 — the lowest among the major CEE recipients:

| Country | Absorption rate |
|---------|----------------|
| Estonia | 97.1% |
| Poland | 95.2% |
| Lithuania | 93.5% |
| Czechia | 91.3% |
| Latvia | 90.8% |
| Hungary | 88.7% |
| Slovakia | 84.1% |
| Croatia | 81.2% |
| Bulgaria | 78.9% |
| **Romania** | **72.4%** |

The 25-percentage-point gap between Romania and Estonia represents billions of euros of available investment that Romania did not translate into projects. The causes are structural: weak project preparation capacity, under-staffed managing authorities, procurement irregularities requiring fund returns, and difficulty co-financing projects from a constrained national budget.

This matters enormously for the growth outlook. Romania has one of the highest GDP-per-infrastructure-euro potential returns in the EU — it has the most to gain from cohesion investment. Its consistent failure to absorb these funds is a self-inflicted constraint on potential growth.

*→ Chart: `05e_eu_funds.png`*

## 6. Sectoral Highlights

### 6.1 Industry: Strong but Concentrated

Romanian industrial production has broadly tracked EU trends, with periods of significant outperformance — particularly in 2017–2019. The base is relatively diversified: automotive, petrochemicals, food processing, textiles, and a growing electronics and machinery sub-sector.

The automotive cluster is the most significant. Dacia (owned by Renault-Nissan) produces over 300,000 vehicles annually from its Mioveni plant and is Romania's single largest exporter. Ford Otosan's Craiova plant adds further capacity. The transition to electric vehicles poses both an opportunity (Romania produces cells and components through partnerships) and a risk (the ICE drivetrain expertise embedded in the supply chain faces obsolescence).

*→ Chart: `06a_industrial_production.png`*

### 6.2 IT Sector: The Bright Spot

Romania has developed one of the region's strongest IT and software development industries. The sector employs over 150,000 people, contributes an estimated 6–7% of GDP, and has attracted significant outsourcing investment from Western multinationals. Bucharest, Cluj-Napoca, Iași, and Timișoara host dense clusters of software companies and R&D centres.

High-technology exports as a share of manufactured exports have risen but remain below Czech and Polish levels, reflecting that Romania's IT strength is primarily in services (exported as services, not goods) rather than high-tech manufacturing.

*→ Chart: `06b_hightech_exports.png`*

### 6.3 Energy

Romania's electricity generation mix is notably diversified by regional standards: roughly 25–30% hydro, 18% nuclear (Cernavodă's two CANDU units), with coal declining to around 16% and wind plus solar rising to a combined 20–22%. Romania is one of the few EU members with both domestic oil production (Petrom) and gas reserves — though both are in decline.

The energy transition agenda is complex. Romania has committed to closing its coal plants (Oltenia), but the replacement capacity (additional nuclear units, offshore Black Sea wind) requires capital investment that depends on the same constrained fiscal environment.

*→ Chart: `06d_electricity_mix.png`*

### 6.4 Agriculture: Potential Squandered

Romania has the EU's second-largest arable land area (after France) yet remains a net importer of processed food. Cereal yields are consistently 30–50% below German levels — the gap reflects the dominance of smallholder farming, under-investment in irrigation (Romania had extensive irrigation under communism; most of it has since deteriorated), limited use of precision agriculture, and fragmented land ownership structures.

The agricultural sector's large workforce (roughly 20% of employment against a 4% GDP share) is a proxy for disguised unemployment and structural poverty in rural areas.

*→ Chart: `06e_cereal_yield.png`*

## 7. Regional Disparities: A Country of Extremes

No analysis of Romania is complete without confronting the scale of its internal regional inequality. The gap between Bucharest and the rest of the country is among the widest in the EU.

### 7.1 NUTS2 Divergence

GDP per capita (PPS) as a percentage of the EU27 average, by region (~2022):

| Region | % of EU27 avg |
|--------|--------------|
| Nord-Est | 28% |
| Sud-Vest Oltenia | 34% |
| Sud-Est | 37% |
| Sud-Muntenia | 38% |
| Nord-Vest | 52% |
| Centru | 57% |
| Vest | 67% |
| **Bucuresti-Ilfov** | **162%** |

Nord-Est — home to Iași, the second-largest city — sits at **28% of the EU average**. This is below Bulgaria's poorest regions and on a par with some of the most deprived areas of Northern Macedonia. Bucharest-Ilfov, meanwhile, at 162%, is wealthier than Berlin, Stockholm, or Amsterdam on this measure.

The ratio between Romania's richest and poorest regions is approximately **5.8:1**. For comparison, the equivalent ratio in Poland is around 3.5:1, in France 2.5:1.

*→ Charts: `07a_nuts2_gdp.png`, `07c_nuts2_comparison.png`*

### 7.2 Divergence, Not Convergence

The internal regional story is not one of convergence — Bucharest has been pulling away from the rest for two decades. The capital captures the lion's share of FDI, EU-funded infrastructure investment, skilled labour (both domestic and return migrants), and government employment. Every major multinational entering Romania heads to Bucharest. Every talented graduate from Nord-Est or Oltenia faces a stark choice: Bucharest or abroad.

*→ Chart: `07b_bucharest_divergence.png`*

### 7.3 Population Haemorrhage Outside the Capital

Regional population change between 2007 and 2022 tells the story bluntly: every region except Bucharest-Ilfov (which gained roughly 80,000 people) lost population. Sud-Muntenia lost an estimated 200,000; Nord-Est 180,000. Rural depopulation in Moldavia (Nord-Est), Oltenia, and Dobrogea is severe enough to threaten the viability of basic services — schools, hospitals, local government — in hundreds of communes.

*→ Chart: `07d_regional_population.png`*

## 8. Social Indicators: Growth Without Inclusion

### 8.1 Poverty

Romania's at-risk-of-poverty or social exclusion rate (AROPE) stood at **27.9% of the population in 2024**. This has declined markedly from the 36% seen in 2020 — wage increases and pension hikes have lifted many above the statistical poverty threshold. But at 27.9%, Romania remains one of the two or three EU member states with the highest poverty rates (alongside Bulgaria at 30.3% and, depending on the year, Romania or Bulgaria trading the bottom position).

The EU27 average is 21%. Czechia achieves 11.3% — Romania's rate is more than double.

*→ Chart: `08a_poverty.png`*

### 8.2 Inequality

The Gini coefficient — zero being perfect equality, 100 being maximum inequality — has improved notably in recent years, falling from 34.3 in 2021 to **28.0 in 2024**. This rapid improvement reflects primarily the compression of wage distribution at the bottom due to aggressive minimum wage increases. Whether this improvement is durable or reverses as fiscal consolidation constrains public wages will be an important test.

Romania's Gini of 28.0 now sits closer to the EU average of 29.4 than at any point in recent history — one of the few genuinely positive recent developments.

*→ Chart: `08b_gini.png`*

### 8.3 Education: A Crisis in Progress

Early school leaving — the share of 18–24 year olds who have not completed upper secondary education and are not in further training — stands at **16.8% in Romania in 2024**, nearly double the EU27 average of 9.4%. The EU's 2030 target is 9%. Romania's rate was 16.3% in 2023 and 15.6% in 2022 — it is rising, not falling.

| Country | 2022 | 2023 | 2024 |
|---------|------|------|------|
| Romania | 15.6%| 16.6%| 16.8%|
| EU27    |  9.6%|  9.6%|  9.4%|
| Poland  |  4.7%|  3.7%|  4.1%|
| Czechia |  6.2%|  6.4%|  5.4%|

Each percentage point represents thousands of young Romanians permanently locked out of formal employment prospects. The rising trend, against a falling EU average, suggests that the structural drivers — rural poverty, school infrastructure deficits, economic necessity to work rather than study — are intensifying, not abating.

*→ Chart: `08c_early_school_leaving.png`*

### 8.4 Healthcare: Europe's Most Underfunded System

Romania spends approximately **$902 per capita on healthcare** (current prices, 2022) — compared to $2,431 in Czechia and $6,226 in Germany. On a PPP-adjusted basis the gap is somewhat smaller, but Romania's health spending as a share of GDP (approximately 6%) remains the lowest in the EU.

The consequences are visible in outcomes:

**Life expectancy** (2022): Romania 75.2 years, Czechia 78.9 years, Germany 80.6 years. The 3.7-year gap to Czechia is exceptional between two EU member states.

**Infant mortality** (2022): Romania 5.4 per 1,000 live births, Czechia 2.2. Romania's rate is more than double that of its nearest peer and 4–5 times the rate of the best-performing EU states (Scandinavia).

The healthcare system suffers from chronic underfunding, severe staff shortages caused by emigration of doctors and nurses (an estimated 14,000 Romanian doctors work abroad), crumbling infrastructure particularly outside major cities, and endemic informal payments that effectively constitute a regressive private tax on the sick.

*→ Charts: `08d_health_expenditure.png`, `08e_life_expectancy.png`, `08f_infant_mortality.png`*

## 9. Structural Problems: A Synthesis

Romania's challenges are interconnected. Understanding them requires looking at the system, not just the individual indicators.

### 9.1 The Fiscal Trap

The fiscal deficit is not simply the result of irresponsible politicians, though there has been political irresponsibility. It emerges from a structural trap: Romania has the revenue base of a lower-middle-income country (28.7% of GDP) with the spending commitments of a European welfare state. The gap is paid for by debt. The debt accumulation raises borrowing costs. Higher borrowing costs widen the deficit further. The escape requires either dramatically higher revenue collection (politically difficult, administratively demanding, slow to materialise) or dramatic spending cuts (politically near-impossible given the pension and wage commitments already baked in).

The 2025–2030 fiscal consolidation path agreed with the EU Commission targets deficit reduction of roughly 0.5–0.7% of GDP per year. At that pace, Romania would not return to the 3% threshold until 2030–2031 at the earliest — and that assumes no recessions, no political reversals.

### 9.2 The Demographic Time Bomb

The population decline is not yet fully priced into Romania's fiscal projections. The pension system's sustainability calculations assume a working-age population that no longer exists. Every year of continued emigration — particularly the emigration of the young and skilled — reduces the future tax base that must service the growing public debt and fund the growing pension bill. There is a self-reinforcing dynamic at work: deteriorating public services (due to fiscal pressure) drive more emigration (particularly of those with options), which reduces the revenue base, which worsens public services.

### 9.3 Infrastructure Deficit

Romania has approximately 1,000 km of motorways — the lowest density among EU member states of comparable size. Rail infrastructure is largely unreformed since the communist era. Internet connectivity in rural areas lags. The physical infrastructure gap is both a direct constraint on productivity and a contributing factor to regional divergence and emigration.

The 2021–2027 NRRP (National Recovery and Resilience Plan) allocates €29 billion to Romania — the largest per-capita allocation in the bloc. If absorbed effectively, it could meaningfully accelerate infrastructure development. But Romania's track record on EU fund absorption (72% in the previous period) casts a long shadow over this prospect.

### 9.4 Institutional Capacity

Underlying many of Romania's failures is a question of state capacity: the ability of the public administration to design, implement, and manage complex programmes. Weak project management in managing authorities, procurement systems vulnerable to abuse, a judiciary that is functional but slow, and a civil service that has lost much of its best talent to the private sector and abroad — these factors collectively explain why EU funds go unabsorbed, why infrastructure projects are delivered years late and over budget, and why tax collection underperforms its potential.

## 10. Outlook and Scenarios

### Baseline

The European Commission and IMF project Romania's growth at **2.0–2.5% in 2025–2026**, conditional on fiscal consolidation proceeding broadly as planned. Public debt is projected to reach 58–60% of GDP by 2027 — approaching the Maastricht ceiling — before stabilising if consolidation holds. Inflation is expected to remain elevated at 5–6% through 2025 before returning to the 3–4% range.

This baseline assumes no major political disruptions, continued EU fund flows, and a stable external environment.

### Optimistic Scenario

If Romania implements genuine fiscal consolidation (reducing the deficit to 4–5% by 2026), accelerates NRRP absorption, and benefits from nearshoring flows as Western firms diversify supply chains, growth could re-accelerate to 3–4% by 2027. The IT sector and green energy transition offer genuine structural tailwinds. Wage convergence, if matched by productivity, is a feature rather than a bug.

### Pessimistic Scenario

If fiscal consolidation stalls (politically likely given the coalition dynamics in Bucharest), credit agencies downgrade Romanian debt to high-yield territory, borrowing costs spike, and the deficit-debt dynamic becomes self-reinforcing. Continued emigration of skilled labour accelerates skill shortages, constraining the very growth needed to resolve the fiscal problem. Regional depopulation crosses thresholds making social services non-viable in the one-third of the country most in need.

### What History Suggests

Countries with structural deficits of this magnitude and revenue bases this thin have tended to require either external programme conditionality (IMF/EU bailout, as Romania experienced in 2009) or sustained multi-year political consensus for reform. Romania's political landscape in 2024–2025 — fragmented, populist pressures from multiple directions, coalition government with conflicting incentives — is not historically associated with sustained fiscal discipline. The risk of muddling through, with gradual deterioration rather than crisis or reform, is the most probable path.

## Key Takeaways

1. **Romania has converged economically** — GDP per capita at 77% of the EU average is a genuine achievement, unprecedented in the country's modern history.

2. **The fiscal situation is unsustainable** — a 9.3% deficit in a non-recessionary year, with no credible short-term path to correction, is the single most acute macroeconomic risk.

3. **Demographics are a slow-motion crisis** — 4 million fewer people than in 1990, with continued outflow of the young and skilled, undermines every aspect of long-term sustainability.

4. **Social outcomes lag economic growth** — 28% poverty rate, 17% early school leaving, life expectancy 3+ years below peers, infant mortality double regional peers: growth has not trickled down equitably.

5. **Regional divergence is accelerating** — the 5.8x gap between Bucharest and Nord-Est is not narrowing; it is widening.

6. **Institutional capacity is the binding constraint** — Romania consistently underperforms its potential on every dimension that requires state action: tax collection, EU fund absorption, infrastructure delivery, public service quality.

7. **The window for adjustment is real but narrowing** — the combination of EU fund access, still-manageable debt levels, and positive growth momentum gives Romania tools that many comparator countries lack. Whether the political system can use them is the central open question.
